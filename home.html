Introduction to Shark Attacks

Contrary to what Hollywood would have us believe, experts insist that sharks do not actively seek out humans as prey. In fact, you're 75 times more likely to get killed by a lightning strike than a shark attack, according to the Florida Museum of Natural History. Yet, a number of surfers and swimmers have serious brushes with sharks on global beaches every year, and some come away from the skirmishes with fewer limbs than they had before.

Most of the incidents in the Global Shark Attack File have nothing to do with predation on humans. Some accidents are motivated by a displacement or territorial behavior when a shark feels threatened; still others are the result of the shark responding to sensory predatory input (i.e., overwhelmed by the presence of many fishes) and environmental conditions (murky water), which may cause them to respond in a reflexive response to stimuli. Sharks also exhibit curiosity and may investigate unknown or unfamiliar objects; they learn by exploring their environment, and - lacking hands - they use their mouths and teeth to examine unfamiliar objects.

Shark Attack Map

This map was made with Plot.ly (python) for the "Data Visualization" course, taught by Prof. Kevin Hartman.

Based on the shark attack data from 1800-2016, I tried to give some insights regarding the location of the shark attacks.

It was found that beaches in Florida are the most dangerous in terms shark attacks. Among all the shark attacks in the U.S., 50% of them happened in Florida, where 52% were in Volucia and Brevard Counties

Here are the steps I took in making this interactive map:

  1. Download data and convert it to CSV;
  2. clean data with Python (count number of attacks by county);
  3. Utilize Python Geopy to pin the county locations.
  4. Plot by Plot.ly.

Breaking News

Although the overall number of reported shark attacks around the world has gone up, a person individual risk of being bitten by a shark has plummeted, according to a new study.


In June 2016, a 60-year-old woman diving at a popular spot in Perth, Australia, was attacked and killed by a large shark. Her friend noticed commotion in the water but by the time he pulled her out to safety it was too late. Her wounds proved fatal. Nearby fishermen said the shark was larger than their 17-foot boat.


In May 2016, 29-year-old Ben Gerring was surfing near Perth, Australia, when his leg was bitten off by a great white shark. He died of his injuries later that week. Local officials caught and killed a 15-foot shark soon after, but weren't sure if it was the same one that attacked Gerring. Swimmers were warned to steer clear of the area.
